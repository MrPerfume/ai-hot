There is a bit of a theme emerging in transportation — and really every industry: AI is creating jobs for some at the loss of others.
General Motors
, for instance,
laid off more than 10% of its IT department
, or about 600 salaried employees — in a deliberate skills swap.

This won’t translate into a one-to-one exchange, which means there will likely be a net-negative job loss.

But GM insists it is hiring and those layoffs have made room for it to recruit IT people with AI-focused backgrounds.
The most sought-after capabilities are AI-native development, data engineering and analytics, cloud-based engineering, agent and model development, prompt engineering, and new AI workflows.

In practical terms, GM is looking for people who know how to build with AI from the ground up — designing the systems, training the models, and engineering the pipelines — not just use AI as a productivity tool.
Those AI job losses are mounting in the automotive sector.
